Acid Rain in America

What is Acid Rain?

Acid rain is one of the most direct consequences of large-scale pollution. When we see large smokestacks rising out of industrial areas, they are typically releasing huge clouds of smoke containing various dangerous chemical compounds. Two of the most common compounds released are sulfur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx). These compunds, when combined with water and oxygen, will create sulfuric and nitric acids that enter our waterways and eventually become precipitation in the form of rain, snow, fog or even dust.

Consequences of Acid Rain

A low pH level in precipitation has a wide range of effects on plant and animal life, ocean acidity and human health. As acid rain makes its way into waterways, certain aquatic species will no longer be able to survive in the changed environment. In addition to killing off some species, some of these toxins will be transferred up the food chain, affecting animals in our diets. In areas affected by acid rain, trees and plants are often seen decaying rapidly. This is a result of the acid removing nutrients from the surrounding soil and surface area of the plant.

Legislation

In 1990, the EPA established the Acid Rain Program, a cap-and-trade system for sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide transmissions. In a cap-and-trade system, pollution sites are only able to emit a certain amount of each pollutant (also known as a cap). Sites that install systems to reduce emissions are able to trade their excess pollutant allowance to other parties. This program both cuts down on total emissions as well as providing financial incentives to parties who install emission reducing systems.

The Dataset

For this visualization, I am using the EPA's Clean Air Dataset which compiles air quality measurements from over 100 monitoring sites in the United States. The measurements included date back to the late 1980's, right before the EPA introduced the Acid Rain Program. By using the slider in the upper right-hand corner, you can see the trend of sulfur dioxide and nitrate pollution in the air over the years. Also presented is a graph of hydrogen ion concentration in rainwater taken from the Hubbard Brook experimental forest in New Hampshire. This forest was set aside for hydrologic research in 1955 and is an important source of water quality readings. The hydrogen ion concentration in rainwater is directly affected by the water's pH level. Elevated levels of hydrogen ions is an indicator of low pH, which means a higher acidity level.
